Research

I work at the interface of condensed matter physics and quantum information science, and am particularly interested in non-equilibrium quantum systems. My research focuses on how quantum mechanics gives rise to surprising collective phenomena at macroscopic scales, and on finding ways to harness these effects in new technological applications, such as quantum computers, or quantum sensors.

Quantum sensing

Entangled many-body states can push measurement precision beyond classical limits. I am interested in how to develop sensing protocols that achieve this using only the coarse tools available in experimental many-body systems, particularly cold atoms and solid-state platforms.

Recently I have focussed on spin squeezing, a particular class of protocols for generating highly entangled states for quantum metrology. Squeezing has previously been studied due to simplicity, and realisability in AMO systems, however I have focussed on its scalability in non-ideal settings: can squeezing remain effective in systems of large numbers of atoms, molecules or colour centres? My work explores the thermodynamic stability of squeezing protocols, and in particular whether disorder and decoherence present a dynamical obstruction to squeezing at scale.

Quantum chaos, ergodicity breaking, and localization

A glass of pure water has $\sim 10^{25}$ molecules in it, and yet can be described for all practical purposes by a single quantity: its temperature. Our ability to describe such a complex, chaotic system so simply is due to "thermalisation": systems gravitate towards a simplified description, based on statistical mechanics, and forget unimportant details about their history.

Remarkably, in some cases, the assumptions of quantum statistical mechanics are violated. One such example is many-body localization, where strong disorder causes a quantum system to fail to thermalize, retaining memory of its initial conditions indefinitely.

My research has focused on developing an understanding of when and how localization happens, when it is stable, when it is not, and how it breaks down in critical systems.

Topology in quantum dynamics

Certain crystalline solids are known to have topological phases, in which their electromagnetic response is quantised. It was for this surprising discovery that the 2016 Nobel prize was awarded. Topological quantisation is appealing as it leads to physics that is extremely robust to the presence of perturbations, disorder, thermal excitation and interactions. Secondly, it is also known that dynamical systems can also exhibit phases and transitions between them. A natural question is: can these dynamical phases also exhibit topological quantisation?

I am interested in the landscape and classification of topological driven quantum phases, and in finding ways that this extremely robust physics can be used to develop robust protocols, e.g. for robust quantum state preparation or information processing.

Quantum and low dimensional materials

Engineered solids now let us build electronic systems to order. Stacking atomically thin sheets with a small twist produces moiré materials, in which a long-wavelength pattern reshapes how electrons move and interact, giving rise to strongly correlated phases — including superconductivity — that are absent in the constituent materials. These platforms are remarkably tunable: the same device can be dialled between very different electronic phases.

I am interested in what new physics these systems realise, and in how external drives, such as light, can be used to switch order on and off. My work has uncovered exotic regimes of magnetic breakdown at experimentally accessible fields, identified superconductivity in a tunable moiré quasicrystal, and studied how superconductors respond to light — a route towards using a material's intrinsic properties for signal transduction.

Quasiperiodicity, fractality, hyperuniformity and disorder

In solid state physics the simplest model of many solids is the perfect crystal. In practice, deviations from this perfect arrangement are unavoidable. Sometimes these deviations are random, but not always: in incommensurate systems, two regular patterns are superimposed with no common period. A prominent example is the moiré pattern produced by two graphene sheets rotated slightly with respect to one another.

My work in this area has uncovered new critical points and phase transitions that sit between the well-studied clean and randomly-disordered limits. A striking example arises in the Hofstadter model, where we showed the critical physics is extremely exotic and controlled by a chaotic renormalisation group flow.
